\section{Introduction}

% 1. Motivate counterfactual feedback as a learning tool
Providing feedback on student work is an essential aspect of education as it helps learners identify specific areas in which their knowledge or process can be improved (Hattie et.al). In providing feedback for complex, open-ended work, instructors often utilize rubrics that break down how student work scores across a set of criteria. In theory, any rubric-based feedback implies the existence of counterfactual work, a modified version of the graded work that would receive a different score according to the rubric. To give students an opportunity to see a counterfactual version of work, instructors may share an example work that scored highly on the rubric, demonstrating to students an ideal version of work for a given assignment. While these exemplary works may provide helpful ideals for students to strive towards, the example work may be considerably different from the student's work in terms of style, topic, or sophistication. This qualitative gap between the scored example work and their own work can make it difficult for students to translate how their work could be improved and limit the learning effectiveness of the provided feedback. Ideally, when receiving feedback, a student would receive a personalized counterfactual version of their work, showing an alternative version of their work that demonstrates minimal changes for their work to be improved.

% 2. Expose the difficulty in counterfactual feedback (tada llms are cool)
While individualized counterfactual feedback provides a powerful learning opportunity for students, manually crafting such feedback is time-intensive, which makes it infeasible to deliver at scale. However, when student work is expressed in textual form, recent advances in natural language processing provide opportunities for automation. In particular, recent work explores approaches that leverage the capabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs) to generate counterfactual examples across a wide range of tasks, such as story rewriting (Chen 2022), augmenting training data, or model interpretability (cites). These findings suggest that the generative and instruction-following capabilities of LLMs make them well-suited for automating the generation of counterfactual feedback. 

% 3. Sketch existing approaches, and gaps in existing work which motivates our contributions
Existing LLM-based approaches to automated counterfactual generation must address a central challenge in designing a counterfactual generation approach: balancing the tradeoff of task performance and similarity to the original factual text. Since these two objectives are often in opposition, a variety of approaches exist to address this tradeoff. Some approaches explicitly optimize a language model for this joint objective (cites), creating a model that explicitly encodes the tradeoff in its weights. Another common pattern across approaches is to decompose the problem into identification and replacement steps, where one model identifies words of the factual text that should be revised and a second replaces the identified words for the counterfactual task. The models used in each step are either LLMs trained for the specific step on a given task (cite) or a large pretrained LLMs. This decomposition-based approach implicitly enforces similarity by the assumption that changing only the words with maximum influence will minimize the total number of words changed, enforcing similarity. 

While existing approaches of counterfactual generation provide promising directions towards automating counterfactual feedback, significant work remains to bridge these approaches into systems that can be used in a real classroom Filighera (cite). One factor that causes these approaches to fall short is the tight coupling between task alignment and similarity control. Most approaches to counterfactual generation require training task-specific system components, such as classifiers or control modules that jointly define what constitutes a valid output and how the counterfactual examples are generated. As a result, adapting a new task often requires retraining or redesigning the system, which limits the portability of these approaches to classroom environments that don't contain sufficient training examples or easy portability.  A second related limitation is that most existing work frames counterfactual generation in terms of nominal or categorical labels, such as sentiment, topic, or toxicity. In these settings, success if defined by crossing a decision boundary, and counterfactuals are evaluated by whether they achieve a label flip. This framing obscures the notion of degree or direction of change, as there is no representation of how much an example improves on a particular axis.  Consequently, existing methods optimize for minimal boundary-crossing edits rather than structured, incremental improvement. However, rubric-based feedback is inherently ordinal, and meaningful feedback requires nuanced adjustments rather than broad, categorical shifts. When counterfactual generation is restricted to coarse-grained labels of correctness, the resulting feedback may be technically valid but pedagogically limited.

% Explicitly listing our contributions: outlining what we do in the rest of the paper
\textbf{Contributions} In this work, we propose a counterfactual generation framework that decouples similarity control from counterfactual task alignment. For similarity control, we introduce $\beta$-Hindsight control, an inference-time LLM steering approach which recovers the stochastic state of an LLM implied by a factual example to control the LLM during generation. For counterfactual task alignment, we train a mid-sized, instruction-tuned LLM through a direct preference optimization (DPO) to generate examples aligned to an in-context rubric. 

We evaluate our approach using a dataset of student writing summaries graded on a rubric to assess the quality of the summary across diverse criteria. Our evaluation demonstrates our approach allows for tuneable control between task validity and faithfulness to original student work on an ordinal dataset. We further perform an ablation study on prompt design to highlight the required conditions needed to use the stochastic signals recoverable from LLM logits.


\section{Background}
\subsection{Gumbel-Max Trick}\label{sec:trick}

In our approach to generating counterfactual student answers, we leverage the Gumbel-Max trick, which we briefly detail below. The Gumbel-Max trick is a procedure to sample from an arbitrary categorical distribution that involves adding noise sampled from a Gumbel distribution \cite{Maddison2014AS}. The samples from this procedure are shown to be equivalent to directly sampling from the categorical distribution, but the trick has an added benefit of decomposing the sampling into a reusable noise artifact, which can be reused to replay the randomness of the sampling process. 
% This decomposition has properties that have allowed for its use in a variety of other scenarios in machine learning, most notably in inspiring the Gumbel-Softmax procedure, which permits gradient-based optimization over categorical variables TODO cite.

We formally introduce this trick by first stating the output of an LLM, the probability of a next token, expressed in terms of the logits the model produces.

$$P(Y = v) = \frac{\exp(\ell_v)}{\sum_{v' \in V} \exp(\ell_{v'})}$$

Here $Y$ is a random variable for the next output token; $V$ is the model's vocabulary, the set of all tokens the model is trained to output; and $\ell_v$ are the logits, or score, for each token $v \in V$. In our work, we consider logits produced by transformer-based, neural language models that are conditioned on a prompt and previously generated tokens.
% This equation is a simple softmax formulation to convert the un-normalized scores into a formal probability distribution over likely next tokens.

Since we express $Y$ in terms of logits, we can use the Gumbel-Max trick to sample the next token instead of sampling from $P(Y)$ directly. In the trick, we draw a noise sample from the Gumbel distribution for each token, which is in turn added to the logits. We then take the maximum perturbed logit index as the sample.
$$ y = \arg\max_{v \in V} (\ell_v + g_v)$$
Here, $g_v \sim \text{Gumbel}(0,1)$ is an independent draw from a standard Gumbel distribution. This simple procedure is mathematically equivalent to directly sampling from $P(Y=v)$. Moreover, the Gumbel noise values can be stored to preserve the random state of the sampling process, which is critical in our counterfactual sampling setting. 

In our approach, we explore using the recovered Gumbel noise for counterfactual generation, as it allows us to hold the exogenous component of sampling fixed after applying an intervention to a language model--the logit generation process. In other words, we record the sampled Gumbel values, alter the language model, and then replay the Gumbel-Max trick with the recorded, ``factual'' noise values on the counterfactual logits. Recent work has explored the use of Gumbel noise recovery and replay to analyze language model bias \cite{chatzi2025counterfactual} or the relative effect of various steering techniques \cite{ravfogel2025gumbel}.

\subsection{Hindsight Gumbel Sampling}

As we detail in the previous section, the Gumbel-Max trick provides a mechanism to preserve and replay the random noise used in discrete random processes. However, in practice, we often do not have access to the random process and only have access to observations from an implied random process. For example, the random process that generates student writing is the students themselves, not a computational system that produces logits. When the true random process is inaccessible, we use a proxy for the random process and recover the noise implied by the observations under that proxy. \citet{ravfogel2025gumbel} refer to the task of recovering noise from a proxy as ``Hindsight Gumbel Sampling''. In their work, the authors use an LLM as a proxy for the textual data generation process and outline an algorithmic procedure to recover Gumbel noise implied by data. 

In our work, we introduce a practical algorithm for Hindsight Gumbel Sampling that allows for efficient, posterior Gumbel noise recovery from ground-truth, reference text. Furthermore, we investigate leveraging this recovered noise as a control signal for the downstream task of student counterfactual example generation. To our knowledge, we present the first exploration of Hindsight Gumbel Sampling on a downstream contrastive editing task with immediate practical implications.

First Gumbel noise from a ``factual'' text is recovered through an algorithmic procedure on the base, untreated language model. We detail a sampling-based procedure for counterfactual feedback generation, summarized in Algorithm~\ref{alg:gumbel-cf-short}. \al{algorithm notation is a bit different (using $\ell(v)$ instead of $\ell_v$) so should make consistent} To generate counterfactual feedback, we recover noise that reproduces the original factual output under a base model and replay the noise while sampling from an intervened model.

To recover the Gumbel noise that corresponds to each token of a student's answer, we construct a noise vector $\mathbf{g} = \{g_v\}_{v \in V}$ that satisfies the condition: \begin{equation} \label{max-condition}
    \ell_{y^*_t} + g_{y_t} > \ell_v + g_v\  \forall v \in V, v \ne y_t
\end{equation}
Here $y^*_t$ is the index in the LLM output vocabulary $V$ of the observed student answer token at autoregressive timestep $t$, thus $\ell_{y^*_t}$ is the logit corresponding to the factual, ground-truth token. \al{I feel like this previous sentence could be simplified} This condition guarantees that, upon applying the Gumbel-Max trick, the observed factual token $y^*_t$ is selected from the Gumbel-Max prediction.

In order to satisfy Condition~\ref{max-condition}, we form a noise vector by first sampling the target noise from a lower-truncated Gumbel distribution. 
$$
\mathrm{LowerTruncG}(\tau_{min}) \coloneqq \mathcal{L}\bigl(G \mid G > \tau_{min}\bigr),
$$ \al{never defined $\mathcal{L}$}
Here $\quad G \sim \mathrm{Gumbel}(0,1)$ is noise drawn from a standard Gumbel, and $\tau_{min}$ denotes the lower bound. In our approach, we compute $\tau_{min}$ as the difference between the ground truth token logit $\ell_{y_t^*}$ and the maximum observed logit $\ell_{max}$ for each timestep. \al{could include this in the formula, i.e., $\tau_{min} = \ell_{max} - \ell_{y^*_t}$} \al{and motivate why - ``so that the augmented logit for the ground-truth token will be greater than the logit for any other token''}

\subsubsection{Task Validity}
We consider a generated counterfactual valid if it achieves the desired target output. \al{phrasing is a bit vague} Existing work on counterfactual generation evaluates tasks with nominal data (e.g. sentiment or topic classification) and thus reports exact match measures of validity, such as flip rate \cite{ross-etal-2021-explaining}. In our work, we instead consider ordinal scores and therefore report a Quadratic Weight Kappa (QWK) as a validity metric. Specifically, we report the QWK agreement between the desired, intervened counterfactual score $z'$ and an estimated score provided by a scoring model $s(y_c)=\hat{z}'$. QWK compares weighted observed disagreement to the expected by chance, producing a score in $[-1,1]$ where high values indicate higher task validity. 
\mh{If we prefer to report correlation or bias instead, the trends are similar. I have the values}

\begin{equation*}
Validity \;=\; 1 \;-\;
\frac{\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sum_{j=1}^{k} w_{ij} \, O_{ij}}
{\displaystyle \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sum_{j=1}^{k} w_{ij} \, E_{ij}},
\end{equation*}
Here $O$ is the observed confusion matrix, $E$ is the expected counts under independence, $w_{ij}$ is the standard quadratic rating $w_{ij} = \frac{(i-j)^{2}}{(k-1)^{2}}$, and $k=4$ in our dataset. \al{might not need to show the formula to save space}

\subsection{Gumbel Noise is an Effective Control Signal}
In Table~\ref{tab:metrics} we report the results of our approach, $\beta$-hindsight sampling compared to baseline sampling approaches. \al{The table is a bit big. I think you don't need to break down by topic for this table, and then can do a smaller table later that examines the per-topic performance.} In all cases, we set $\beta=0.1$, which we empirically tuned via linear search on a subset of our dataset. We observe across all settings that our method produces counterfactual outputs that are significantly similar to the original factual student response. The observed similarity trend holds across all training setups, indicating that our approach is robust to model weight interventions. Interestingly, we see that in the \textbf{SFT} setting, the model generates outputs that are very similar to the factual compared to other settings. We suspect that the increased similarity in SFT arises because, during supervised fine-tuning, the model weights are directly updated to produce outputs similar to the factual summaries it is later instructed to adhere to, resulting in a stronger recovered control signal. 

In terms of validity, we see that while our approach generally produces counterfactual outputs with higher validity across criteria, with the notable exception of the \textbf{Main Idea} criterion. The \textbf{Main Idea} criterion evaluates the presence of a clear topic sentence at the beginning of the summary. We hypothesize that lower performance on this metric arises from the quantity of character insertions or deletions required to alter the factual output for this criterion. Since the Gumbel noise signal is applied at a fixed position independent of any desired structural change, we suspect that a misalignment occurs between the relative position of the noise in the factual and counterfactual outputs, which may adversely affect the resulting output. This analysis suggests that an alternative algorithm, which more intelligently aligns the recovered factual token noise with the expected token position in the counterfactual, could result in a more general system robust to a wider variety of output changes.
